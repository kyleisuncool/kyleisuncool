# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Visitors arrive almost entirely through relationship and referral, not search or ads. They're evaluating whether to bring in embedded, high-agency help: founders and operators who don't yet have someone who can hold the full technical-and-strategic picture of what they're building. Three rough registers show up in practice: well-resourced founders/developers who need someone who can operate at founding-team level without equity; small practitioners and early-stage businesses who need real infrastructure (not just a website) built from scratch; and mission-driven, resource-constrained organizations where the relationship matters as much as the deliverable.

The job they're trying to get done on this site: decide, in a few minutes, whether the claimed capability is real. They want to see finished, specific, dated work, not a pitch.

## Product Purpose

A personal practice site that proves the "forward deployment" model by showing it, not describing it: case studies from real embedded engagements, essays that think in public (including the still-unsolved question of what to call this work), and a running build journal of Orion, the vault-and-AI operating system that makes the practice possible. The site is itself evidence of the practice: honest about what's draft, willing to kill a case study rather than publish a dishonest one.

## Brand Personality

Three words: **Embedded, Plainspoken, Rigorous.**

Embedded: first-person, on-the-ground, present-tense confidence, not a detached agency voice. Plainspoken: direct sentences, no marketing inflation, no jargon reached for out of habit. Rigorous: specifics (dates, numbers, named artifacts) carry the argument instead of adjectives; the site shows its own workings rather than polishing over them.

## Anti-references

- **Generic AI/SaaS startup.** No hero + three-icon-card grid + dark CTA band template. No gradient text, no stock "innovation" mood, no genericized coral-on-cream AI-brand look.
- **Corporate consultancy.** No McKinsey-deck seriousness, no faceless institutional third-person voice, no jargon-inflated capability statements.
- **Personal blog/Substack.** Not too informal or low-authority — this still has to read as credible, proof-backed work, not a diary.

## Design Principles

1. **Proof over pitch.** Case studies carry real structure and specifics (scope, timeframe, what shipped), not marketing copy dressed as a case study.
2. **Three registers, one voice.** `/work` reads like a portfolio (quiet confidence, project-forward). `/writing` reads like real publishing (editorial, long-form as a first-class artifact). `/orion` reads like a field log (plain, dated, low-chrome). One shared type and color system holds all three together.
3. **Show the seams.** Draft content is visibly draft. A case study that couldn't be told honestly (HAND Protocol) was dropped rather than faked. The positioning problem itself is published as an essay instead of resolved quietly.
4. **Plainspoken, not hyped.** First person, direct sentences. No inflated capability language, no exclamation-point energy.
5. **Dates and specifics carry authority.** Real numbers and dates over adjectives, on every page, not just case studies.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Standard WCAG AA (contrast, keyboard navigation, semantic headings — one H1 per page). Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`; no motion that can't be disabled.

I have a positioning problem, and I've decided to work it out in public.

Over the past several weeks I've been embedded in three builds at once. A regenerative land project in Texas that needed a pitch deck, a financial model, a design system, and a strategic brief — starting from an amorphous ask with no spec. A healing practice that needed a full booking site with payments and a brand pivot — from scratch. And underneath both, an operating system for organizational memory that I built because the work demanded it.

The work has a shape. You can see it across all three: I don't enter a project as a specialist. I enter as the person who figures out what the project actually needs — and then builds it.

What the work doesn't have is a name.

## The titles that don't fit

**"Web developer"** undersells it by a factor of ten. Websites get built, yes. But the website is usually the fourth or fifth deliverable, after the strategy, the financial model, the design language, and the institutional memory that makes all of them coherent.

**"Creative director"** captures the taste but misses the technical depth. A creative director doesn't stand up payment infrastructure or wire a knowledge system.

**"Consultant"** is the worst one, because it's the closest. Consultants diagnose and recommend. The deliverable is advice — a deck about what *you* should build. My deliverable is the built thing. When I leave, the organization has infrastructure it didn't have before, not a reading assignment.

The nearest honest description is something like: what a founding CTO crossed with a creative director does — except arriving from outside, without equity, and leaving the organization more capable than I found it. That's accurate. It's also fifteen words long, which means it isn't a name.

## Why this matters

You can't sell what you can't name. Every engagement so far has come through relationship and reputation — someone who watched the work happen and pulled me into the next thing. That scales exactly as far as the people who've seen it firsthand.

A stranger reading this page doesn't need my org chart position. They need a moment of recognition: *that fog he's describing — I'm standing in it.* The name is what makes that moment findable.

## The term I keep circling

The phrase I currently use is **forward deployment**. I picked it up from a community of builders in Austin, where it names a military-shaped idea: go ahead of the main force, create order out of chaos, build the systems the mission needs, make the ground ready.

That's closer than anything else. It carries the two things the other titles drop: that I go in *first*, before the organization knows what it needs — and that what comes back is *deployed*, running, real.

But it has problems too. It's jargon to almost everyone. It sounds like logistics. And it describes the movement without describing the person — "forward deployment" is a thing that happens, not someone you can hire.

## The open question

So this essay ends without an answer, on purpose. The name will come from the work — probably from the mouth of someone I've worked with, describing me to someone else. Names given by other people tend to be truer than names you give yourself.

In the meantime, the work continues, and this site documents it. If you read the case studies and find yourself thinking *I know what that is, and I know what I'd call it* — I genuinely want to hear it.

That's not rhetorical. What do you call this?

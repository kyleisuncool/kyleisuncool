Orion wasn't designed. It condensed.

In late May 2026 I embedded with a land development project in Texas — my first engagement of the kind this site documents. Within days the problem announced itself: the project generated more context than any person could hold. Meeting transcripts, vendor research, financial inputs, design decisions, a founding team's worth of relationships, risks that only existed *between* topics. Nobody owned the complete picture. That gap was exactly what I'd been brought in to fill, and I couldn't fill it out of my own head.

So I started writing everything down in one place — plain markdown files in a vault — and put an AI operating layer on top. Seven days later there were forty-one interlinked files functioning as a CRM, a knowledge base, a task system, a strategic brief, and an organizational memory. That's when I understood what it was: not a note-taking system. An operating system for an organization.

## The architecture that emerged

Orion settled into a few load-bearing ideas, none of which I knew in advance:

**Knowledge lives in numbered folders; navigation lives apart from it.** Projects, ongoing areas, reference material, an immutable decision log, an archive. A separate wiki layer — overview, index, operation log — only *reflects* the numbered folders, never holds content of its own. When the two disagree, the numbered folders win, and a single sync ritual runs at the end of every session to bring the reflection current.

**People are a live signal, not an address book.** Every relationship has a page with a contact cadence and a follow-up date. The system flags who's going quiet *before* the silence costs something. In an engagement where the work is mostly relationships, this one feature pays for everything else.

**Decisions are append-only.** Every strategic decision gets a dated file: context, decision, rationale, expected outcome, and a follow-up log as reality responds. Past entries never get edited. Six weeks later, "why did we do it this way?" has an answer with a date on it — which is precisely the institutional memory most young organizations don't keep.

**Some risks don't live in any single project.** The most valuable early catches were signals that ran *across* domains — a concern surfacing in one meeting that quietly contradicted something from another workstream. Those became threads: files that track a cross-cutting question through every hub it touches until it resolves.

**The AI reads and writes the same files I do.** No app between us. The vault is plain text, so the operating layer can ingest a voice memo into it, generate a meeting brief out of it, audit it for dead links and stale pages, and compress its own history when the log grows long. The format is the API.

## Why plain text

Because everything else about the system can change — and has. The vault outlived its first name, several structural reorganizations, and a full overhaul of the dashboard that sits on top of it. The markdown survived every migration untouched. Files sync to every device I own, version cleanly in git, and will still open in fifty years. Any tool — human or AI — can be pointed at them.

Durability wasn't the romantic choice. It was the engineering choice.

## What it proved

Orion was built during and for that first engagement — the vault is the proof of concept for the work itself. The combination of embedded human judgment and AI-maintained institutional memory lets one person hold the complete picture of an organization: its decisions, its people, its risks, its gaps. That used to be a capability only a founding team could have, and only if it was unusually disciplined.

The vault is the tool. The judgment is the service. This journal documents how the tool keeps evolving.

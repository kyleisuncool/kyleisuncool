## What it needed

A practicing healer in Austin — Reiki, Akashic Readings, Sound Healing, Hypnotherapy — with a real client base built on word of mouth, and no infrastructure underneath it. No way to book online. No place that presented the practice with the seriousness it deserved. The work was established; the business layer wasn't.

This is a different problem from the land project. There's no investor to convince, no deck to build. The audience is a person who just heard about her from a friend, opens the site on their phone, and decides in thirty seconds whether this feels trustworthy enough to book.

---

## The work

**Positioning first.** Before any code: what is this practice, in her words, for someone who's never encountered it? The brand pivoted during the engagement as that question got answered — the site's language settled into something that sounds like her, not like a template for "wellness services."

**A full booking flow.** Not a contact form — a booking wizard that walks from service selection through scheduling to payment, with the session menu and pricing surfaced up front so there's no mystery. Stripe handles the money. She gets bookings, not inquiries she has to chase.

**Design that carries the practice.** Calm, warm, unhurried — the site is built to feel like the sessions do. Testimonials from her practice woven through the pages, an About page in her own settled voice.

**Launch-grade plumbing.** Deployed infrastructure, payment keys handled properly, the unglamorous work that makes it a business asset instead of a demo.

---

## What moved

The site is feature-complete and near launch: content settled, testimonials live, pricing surfaced, payments wired end to end. The remaining distance is a launch checklist, not a build.

---

## What I learned

Small doesn't mean simple. A one-practitioner healing business needed positioning strategy, a payment integration, and a brand system — the same categories of work as a multi-acre development project, at different scale. The pattern held: figure out what it actually needs, then build all of it, so the pieces agree with each other.

The other lesson is about sequencing. The build was done for weeks while the content wasn't.

> The hard part was never the code. It was helping someone say what their work is.

That's the part that can't be delegated to a template, and it's most of the value.
